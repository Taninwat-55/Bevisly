Somewhere in a hiring manager's inbox right now, there's a resume from someone who could genuinely do the job. It might even be the best candidate in the batch.

But it won't get read.

Not because the person isn't qualified. Because nothing on their CV screams "shortlist me" to a system that was designed to filter on signals that stopped being meaningful decades ago.

That's the problem with traditional hiring, and it hits junior candidates hardest.

## The CV Tells the Wrong Story

A CV answers one question: *where have you been?*

For a senior engineer with ten years of experience, that question has a useful answer. For a recent graduate, a career switcher, or a self-taught developer? It answers almost nothing.

Junior candidates get filtered out on:

- School prestige
- GPA
- Internship brand names
- Years of experience (for jobs that say "entry level")

None of these things reliably predict whether someone can do the work.

A candidate who studied at a top university and had a summer internship at a big company might be mediocre in the role. A candidate who taught themselves, worked a non-traditional job, and built real things in their own time might be excellent. The CV can't tell you which is which.

## What Proof-of-Work Actually Means

The term comes from Bitcoin. Miners prove they did real computational work to earn the reward. No shortcuts. No credentials substituting for effort.

Applied to hiring, it means one thing: **let candidates show what they can do, not just claim it.**

This is exactly what Bevisly is built around. Instead of uploading a CV and writing a cover letter, a candidate applies by completing a real, scoped task that the employer designed — something that reflects what the first week of the job actually looks like.

The output is what gets reviewed. Not where the person went to school. Not how their resume is formatted. The actual work.

## Why This Matters More for Junior Talent

For senior candidates, the portfolio often speaks for itself. GitHub activity, previous companies, published work — there's usually something tangible to point to.

For junior candidates, there's rarely anything. They're in a catch-22: they can't build experience without a job, but they can't get a job without experience.

Proof tasks break that loop. Every completed submission becomes evidence — a permanent, verifiable record of what someone built, rated, and reviewed. Even if they don't get the role.

Over time, a Bevisly profile becomes a real portfolio: a collection of proof tasks, ratings from employers, and feedback that's grounded in actual work. Not self-reported. Not hypothetical.

## The Employer Side of This

This isn't just better for candidates. It's better for employers too.

Screening CVs is expensive. Reading 200 applications, scheduling phone screens, running take-home assessments — it adds up fast. And for startups and SMEs without a dedicated recruiting team, it's a real drain.

Proof-of-work hiring compresses that. You define the task once. Candidates opt in knowing exactly what's expected. You review the output. The signal is higher, and the volume of noise is lower.

The candidates who complete the task are already self-selecting. They're interested enough to do real work, not just click "Easy Apply."

## The Fairness Argument

There's a reason traditional hiring correlates so heavily with socioeconomic background. Access to prestigious schools, unpaid internships, professional networks — these aren't equally distributed.

Proof-of-work doesn't eliminate every form of bias. But it shifts the primary question from *who do you know and where have you been* to *can you do this specific thing well?*

That's a more defensible basis for a hiring decision. And it opens doors for people who've been shut out by systems that were never designed with them in mind.
